Mr. ‘Fresh Air’ leads Japan to a new era

Dr. Abdulla Al-Madani

Published on April 01, 2006

Following his Liberal Democratic Party’s (LDP) landslide victory in the September 11 general elections, Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi has emerged as one of most powerful leaders in post-war Japan. With 296 seats in the 480-member Lower House of parliament and firm control of the country’s biggest party, he has become the undisputed king of Japanese politics.

Highlighting the election’s result and its consequent impact on Japan’s politics and economy, one must recognize the following points:

First, the result was a personal victory for Koizumi, who had been earlier criticized for his decision to hold a snap election. Numerous observers and politicians, including prominent figures within the LDP, viewed the 63-year-old Koizumi’s decision as a suicidal move. Forces opposed to Tokyo’s foreign policy of supporting the US-led Iraq war, particularly in the Arab/Islamic world, were, of course, happy to receive such an analysis, hoping Koizumi’s defeat would end Japan’s peaceful role in Iraq.

Things, however, went against such wishes and all public opinion polls. Koizumi has got the mandate he needed to go ahead with his ambitious domestic reform agenda. The man, who succeeded Yoshiro Mori, one of Japan’s most unpopular leaders, and whose coming to power in April 2001 was described as ‘fresh air’, has now overwhelming power to shape his country’s future.

Despite this, Koisumi, unlike his Third World counterparts, has no intention to stay in office beyond September 2006 when his current term as prime minister ends, saying the remaining period is enough to place Japan on the reform road and to arrange for a talented successor.

Second, the result was a clear triumph for the LDR, especially with the main opposition party, the Democratic Party of Japan (DPJ), ending up with only 113 seats, 62 seats less than its pre-election standing of 175. The LDP, which has run Japan for nearly all the past 50 years, is now in a position that it has not enjoyed since the 1980s. And with Koisumi having strengthened his personal authority, the LDP may become much stronger, given the Prime Minister’s agenda of rejuvenating the party’s structures, purging its anti-reform old guard, and bringing new life and blood into it.

Third, one of the election’s significant results was an increase in the number of female legislatures in the Lower House from 34 to 43. This has been viewed as an unprecedented development, given the fact that the highest previous record was 39, achieved in 1946 when women were first allowed into parliament. Of the 43 new female lawmakers, 26 belong to the LDP, up from just 9 in the dissolved Lower House, something that is attributed to Koisumi’s strategy of giving female candidates preference on the LDP’s proportional representation list.

Japan still has one of the lowest representations of female politicians in the industrialized world. However, with the current 9 percent female representation, the country is closer to the global average of 16 percent and seems to be set on the road to becoming a more gender-equal democracy. Koisumi, who has so far challenged his country and party’s traditions twice by giving the prestigious portfolio of foreign affairs to women, is expected to include more females in both the cabinet and the LDP leadership.

Fourth, the LDP’s overwhelming success has led to hot debates and several structural changes within the opposition parties, the most notable of which was in the DPJ. Admitting humiliating defeat and failure to challenge the ruling party, the DPJ’s president Katsuya Okada resigned in favour of Seiji Maehara. The new youthful- looking 43-year-old president my reconsider his party’s policies, especially those concerning Japan-US relations. It is worth recalling that one of his predecessor’s failed strategies to attract voters was lashing out harshly at Koizumi for the latter’s pro- Washington foreign policy concerning the Iraq issue.

Fifth, the election’s results have given Japan’s aging citizens new hopes for a stronger economic growth capable of providing them with better security and welfare when they retire. In other words, the government has now the power to pass bills on privatizing the savings and insurance programmes of Japan Post, which has US$ 3 trillion in deposits. This would put postal savings into more sound investments rather than using them as an LDP slush fund for public works projects -which is said to be sources of waste and corruption- and consequently would give a boost to the country’s stagnating economy.
