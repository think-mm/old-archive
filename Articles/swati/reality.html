Reality of “Reality”  

By

Swati Ahmed

“Then if the truth about reality is always in our soul, the soul would be immortal so that you should always confidently try to seek out and recollect what you don’t know at present--that is, what you don’t recollect” (Solomon, 79). This conversation of Socrates with Meno is from “The Meno” by Plato.

Actually, I desired to write a paper on reality, but while searching the Internet, books, and other sources, I sat in disappointment. Nothing seemed clear to me. Then the above lines of Socrates caught my eyes, and I started wondering. If the truth about reality is in my soul, then how come I am not able to recollect it! Why is reality so puzzling to write about?

To tell the truth, in my opinion, everything is real, but my opinion of reality varies in some extent from time to time. For instance, we see lights of dead stars. Those stars are long dead, but the light of the stars take many ‘light years’ to reach our planet, so we see them now. What is real here? Those stars are dead or maybe not because we can see them. To me, both are right. In the stars’ time zone, they are dead, but in our time zone, they are not. In the same way, if there is a glass half filled with water, there is always endless discussion whether the glass is half full or half empty. Bill Cosby, the funnyman, actor, and author also had this question and asked his grandmother. “She had it all figured out, ‘Depends on whether you are pouring or drinking’” (McGeorge, 206). What is the reality here? Not only reality varies in different time zones but also varies on individual perception. Half empty or half full both are real but depends on the one’s perception, and Aristotle’s theory supports this reality, “Change is real and much of reality is subject to change” (Solomon, 83).

Another example of reality dilemma seemed interesting to me. In Dr. Hutto’s lecture on “Language, Thought and Reality”, there is an example on how reality changes.

For example we might say ‘X’ is north of ‘Y’ is true-but this mistake makes sense only if we observe certain convention about directional terms. Would it make sense to say that such statement was true leaving beside such concerns? For example, if we look the perspective of someone who wasn’t located on the earth. What earth would north mean to someone on outer space?” (Hutto, 3).

In addition, I cannot ignore what my favorite philosopher Aristotle thought more about reality. “Aristotle also wanted to determine the nature of reality…………….‘This world, our world is reality’” (Solomon, 81). However, pre-Socratic Greek philosopher Parmenides and Aristotle had totally opposite opinions on the topic “reality”. Robert C. Solomon wrote about Parmenides’s belief on reality. 

He too, was a monist and believed in a single reality, “the one”. Because he shared the assumption that reality must be eternal and unchanging, he came to an astonishing conclusion: This world, the world of our experience can’t be real… We are, at best, living in a kind of illusion, not in reality at all! (Solomon, 54).

If we think of Parmenides’s view on reality then we can relate to the virtual reality of today. Maybe the universes and everything we know of are not real but an illusion. Maybe we are in someone’s virtual reality. Then our life and our world are illusions. If so, then Aristotle’s “our world is reality” theory is not real. If we are really living in somewhat of an illusion, in our perception of a half full water glass, would not we believe that, this ‘illusion life’ is real, and to us it is reality?

In conclusion, I would say that I agree with all the philosophers’ opinions and others doctrines about reality. My belief of reality, and accepting any doctrines of the philosophers about reality, depends on when, where, and what emotional stage I am in. At this moment, my reality is how to write a good paper on reality, but at other times, do I even think consciously of the topic “reality”?
