A reply to the article “Is Marxism Scientific?  by Avijit Roy”

By Hassan Nasir


Dear Mr. Avijit Roy,

I read your response to Ms. Hashem and felt that you had made certain scientifically incorrect claims in your essay. You say that Marxism is “unscientific”. I will try to prove to you that it is the only scientific basis of social science. But before I begin to do so, let me introduce myself briefly. I am with the Communist Workers and Peasants Party in Pakistan and we have been using your excellent article on the myth of Quranic science to educate people about religious propaganda. Our website is at www.geocities.com/cmkp_pk and our yahoogroups is at http://groups.yahoo.com/group/cmkp_pk.


I won’t discuss some points in your original essay which were specific to the response to Ms. Hashem but get straight to your scientific inaccuracies.
First, your entire claim of Marxism being unscientific has ignored a vital component of Marxism as a science. Engels claimed in “Socialism Utopian and Scientific” that Karl Marx made not one but two scientific discoveries. The first was “The Materialist Interpretation of History” and the second was “The Theory of Surplus-Value”. Engels says “These two great discoveries, the materialistic conception of history and the revelation of the secret of capitalistic production through surplus-value, we owe to Marx. With these discoveries, Socialism became a science. The next thing was to work out all its details and relations.”

 http://marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1880/soc-utop/ch02.htm


In your entire response, you only dealt with the Theory of Surplus-Value, thereby ignoring a crucial aspect of the argument that Marxism is a science. Would you care to comment on the scientific validity of the materialist interpretation of history? You can read it in the German Ideology at:


http://marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1845/german-ideology/index.htm


Now let us look at your arguments in relation to the Theory of Surplus Value. You wrote “The essence of Marxist economics consists of showing that worker does not get a return in pay equal to the full 'value' which his labor contributes to the product, since part of the profit is retained by the capitalist. This had been pointed out by all the great economists. Marx's new point was to declare this retention of 'surplus value' illegitimate. The novelty was, therefore emotional (ethical) and social rather than economic/scientific.”


This is completely inaccurate. No economist before Marx had pointed out that “the worker does not get a return in pay equal to the full 'value' which his labor contributes to the product”. Please quote any economist before Marx who had proven this point. It follows that you are entirely incorrect in saying that “Marx's new point was to declare this retention of 'surplus value' illegitimate.”


In fact, in Capital Vol 1 Marx points out that the worker does get the value of the product he sells. The exchange-value of the product he is selling is approximated in his wage and the product he is selling is labour-power. What Marx pointed out was that exploitation was concealed within the “exchange of equivalents” (i.e. equivalent values).That the worker gets the worth of his commodity (i.e. labour-power). But the crucial discovery was that labour-power as a historically specific commodity (which results from “freeing” the labourer from the means of production) and determined in accordance with the “law of value” is a commodity that in turn produces value (the labour theory of value). And the value produced by labour-power is greater than the value of labour-power. The difference between the two is surplus-value.


This is indeed a profound scientific discovery that you have misrepresented.


You continue:
“…in first volume of Das Kapital, he argues that since profit accrues solely from the surplus value extracted from labor, the rate of profit will depend upon the proportion of the labor to fixed capital in the form of machinery and will therefore tend to fall as technological improvements lead to the employment of proportionately less labor.”


Just a small correction, actually Marx spoke about the tendency of the rate of profit to fall in Capital Volume III. In Capital Volume I he only defines the organic composition of capital and looks at its relation to necessary and surplus labour-time and therefore necessary and surplus-value. He also looks at the rate of exploitation and the rate of profit but he only explains the law of declining rate of profit in Capital Vol. III.


You continue “This was plainly untrue even at that time, and Marx himself finally noted the difficulty but set in aside for later treatment, which he never gave it.”


I don’t think you have looked at Capital Vol. III. If you just look at the Table of Contents you will see that Marx goes into painstaking detail over all the “difficulties”. Marx calls these “Counteracting tendencies” and looks at them in detail in Part III. These counteracting tendencies are:


1. Increasing intensity of exploitation
2. Depression of wages below the value of labour-power
3. Cheapening of elements of constant capital
4. Relative overpopulation
5. Foreign trade
6. The increase of stock capital


There has been much empirical study on this law and among best works that I can think of have been produced by people like Paul Sweezy (Monthly Review). Sweezy quite convincingly proves the continuing validity of this law and exposes the inaccurate statistical techniques of leading anti-Marxist theoreticians who attempt to disprove the tendency of the rate of profit to decline.


You continue: “Again let's focus on the second 'law' Marx deduced. Marx concluded that the number of capitalists would contract, and as a country advanced economically under capitalism, it would increasingly polarize the into fewer and fewer capitalists and more and more proletarians. This too, fails completely as recognizable account of real evolution of the west and east Europe.”


A more inaccurate statement could not have been made. First, what you are attempting to describe is called “The Law of Concentration of Capital” and it is statistically easier to verify than the falling rate of profit. Here are just a few statistics to prove the growing concentration of capital in the world today.


· Monopolise of trade: Monopolisation of trade levels out fluctuations and creates a more stable rate of profit for the monopoly capitalists. We live in a world today where the top 1 percent of all Multinational companies account for 70 to 80 percent of global trade (40% of which is merely trade between different MNC’s). The top 15 multinational companies control the world market in 20 key commodities: 90 per cent of the world’s trade in iron ore, wheat, timber, cotton, tobacco, pineapples; 80 per cent of the world’s trade in copper, tea, and coffee; 70 per cent of the world’s trade in rice; and 60 per cent of the world’s trade in oil. The top 5 multinational companies account for 70 per cent of consumer durables, 58 per cent of cars, trucks, and airlines, 55 per cent of aerospace, 53 per cent of electronic components, and 50 per cent of oil, steel, personal computers and media industries (Brar, 1997)


· Monopolise Production and Investment: The picture that emerges in the realm of production and manufacture is not too different from that of world trade. A mere 1 percent of all multinational companies own half the total stock of foreign direct investment (FDI)[1], 80 percent of all international investment, and account for 30 per cent of the world’s output.[2] The top 200 multinational companies employ less than 0.05 per cent of the world’s population but control over a quarter of the world’s Gross Domestic Product[3] (Brar, 1997).
Figures of global income and consumption show that the world’s richest 20 percent control approximately 80 percent of income, trade, lending, saving, and investment.

Even bourgeois economist has ever denied that the development of capitalism results in the concentration of capital. It has been scientifically verified to the extent that it cannot be considered a controversial point anymore.


You continue: “It is obvious that Marx deduced his famous "law of increasing misery" emotionally (hypothetically), not scientifically - in which the more the capital invested and the greater the production the less will be the wages paid for labor. This has also proven to be false.”


Please show me where Marx uses the _expression the “law of increasing misery”. This is an _expression from third rate sociology books that incorrectly ascribe to Marx the theory of the “iron law of wages”. In fact, Marx was a critic of the iron law of wages and the law of increasing misery. His own theory bears nothing in common with these views. What he asserted was that the relative gap (as opposed to the absolute conditions of the proletariat) between the bourgeoisie and the proletariat will continue to widen. Here is my evidence:
“Let us suppose the most favorable case: if productive capital grows, the demand for labor grows. It therefore increases the price of labor-power, wages.


A house may be large or small; as long as the neighboring houses are likewise small, it satisfies all social requirement for a residence. But let there arise next to the little house a palace, and the little house shrinks to a hut. The little house now makes it clear that its inmate has no social position at all to maintain, or but a very insignificant one; and however high it may shoot up in the course of civilization, if the neighboring palace rises in equal of even in greater measure, the occupant of the relatively little house will always find himself more uncomfortable, more dissatisfied, more cramped within his four walls.


An appreciable rise in wages presupposes a rapid growth of productive capital. Rapid growth of productive capital calls forth just as rapid a growth of wealth, of luxury, of social needs and social pleasures. Therefore, although the pleasures of the laborer have increased, the social gratification which they afford has fallen in comparison with the increased pleasures of the capitalist, which are inaccessible to the worker, in comparison with the stage of development of society in general. Our wants and pleasures have their origin in society; we therefore measure them in relation to society; we do not measure them in relation to the objects which serve for their gratification. Since they are of a social nature, they are of a relative nature.”


http://marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1847/wage-labour/ch06.htm


Thus, it is crystal clear from this passage that Marx is talking about the relative poverty of the working class. After an elaborate discussion about the relationship between capital and labour-power Marx concludes:


“We have thus seen that even the most favorable situation for the working class, namely, the most rapid growth of capital, however much it may improve the material life of the worker, does not abolish the antagonism between his interests and the interests of the capitalist. Profit and wages remain as before, in inverse proportion.
If capital grows rapidly, wages may rise, but the profit of capital rises disproportionately faster. The material position of the worker has improved, but at the cost of his social position. The social chasm that separates him from the capitalist has widened.”


http://marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1847/wage-labour/ch08.htm


Thus, as you can clearly see from this passage Marx is concerned about the “social chasm” and not the absolute standard of living of the worker. Thus, capitalism will not perish owing to a moral imperative, or owing to an economic crisis, but owing to the increasing social antagonism between the interests of capital and the interests of labour.
Avijit continues: “He thought Capitalism is bound to collapse, not because it was undesirable but because the 'iron laws' of economic development proved that it was already - over century ago - a brake on the productive forces, so that under it the economy would be unable to expand. But facts proved other wise despite Marx thought it right. And his basic failure has naturally involved those Shetara Hasems who still call themselves Marxists in a cycle of errors.”


Show me the passage from Marx that states that there would come a point in capitalism when the rate of growth would become zero. What Marx said was that capitalism has become a brake on the further development of the productive forces in relation to more advanced relations of production (i.e. socialism). Even Feudalism did not stop developing the economy at the point that it was overthrown by capitalism. It was just that in relation to capitalism it was a brake on the further development of the productive forces, just as capitalism today has become a break on the further development of productive forces in relation to socialism.


Avijit Roy concludes from the various disagreements among Marxists that Marxism is not a science. What he neglects is that the in order to prove that Marxism is a science it is not necessary for communists to agree on everything. It is only necessary for them to agree on two things: The Materialist Interpretation of History, the Theory of Surplus-Value. All the tendencies within the Marxist movement that you have pointed out uphold these basic scientific discoveries of Karl Marx. Therefore, their disagreements are over the best course of action in the creation of a socialist society and not over the fundamental scientific basis of Marxism.


Mr Avijit says “…calling Marxism is scientific, leads us to bizarre extreme, because not all the leading Economists view it as scientific, not all prominent philosopher accepts this philosophy as scientific.”


There was a time when not all the leading scientists of the world accepted that the world was not flat. That didn’t change the fact that the flat Earth theory was utterly incorrect. There was a time when scientists were burnt at the stake, it did not change the laws of motion of celestial bodies. In the Muslim world today most people think that God exists. Does that mean it is scientifically correct? Obviously not. What matters is to what extent does an independent scientific examination reveal the validity of Marxism in relation to the objective world. (Bertrand Russell who was opposed to the liberation of the African colonies and was a known aristocratic racist—aside from also being a great mathematician and writer—was not beyond his own class prejudices). But certainly anyone who has read Marx in relation to Bertrand Russell cannot help but feel the infinite superiority of Marx as an intellectual.
You write “Again, leading Marxist and Communist leaders had mostly been of bourgeois origin. Marx here admits that their motivations are not those normally provided for by Marxism. But what is it that determines that some bourgeois intellectuals should become Marxists while others do not? Shetara Hasem might want to come with a scientific explanation, perhaps?”


Please quote where Marx says that the “motivations” of the bourgeois intellectuals who become Marxists are not “provided for by Marxism”? In fact, Marx talks about the development of the revolutionary intelligentsia in many of his works. To start with here is a quote from the Communist Manifesto:


“Finally, in times when the class struggle nears the decisive hour, the progress of dissolution going on within the ruling class, in fact within the whole range of old society, assumes such a violent, glaring character, that a small section of the ruling class cuts itself adrift, and joins the revolutionary class, the class that holds the future in its hands. Just as, therefore, at an earlier period, a section of the nobility went over to the bourgeoisie, so now a portion of the bourgeoisie goes over to the proletariat, and in particular, a portion of the bourgeois ideologists, who have raised themselves to the level of comprehending theoretically the historical movement as a whole.”


http://marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1848/communist-manifesto/ch01.htm


You wrote: “In fact, Marx's idea of a society which would ensure for everyone fair shares of the goods produced has not a "new" one, and had long been a unfulfilled dream in human insight. In some country, particularly Britain, has produced a large socialist party without much reference to special doctrine of Marx. Even, Dialectic Materialism, for which Marx will be remembered, is not his own creation. Ancient Greek Philosopher, Hiraclitas preached the same philosophy long time ago. Marx describes his system as arising from three sources - the philosophy of German Hegel, the economics of Richardo, ando other British economists, and the social theories of French Socialists like Saint-Simon.”


Is it so hard to understand that Marx made a scientific contribution distinct from Hiraclitas or the utopian socialists? His scientific discovery was not Dialectics. It was not even socialism. It was the foundation of socialism on a scientific footing by discovering two things 1) the Materialist interpretation of history 2) the Theory of Surplus-Value. Find me one person in history that elaborated these ideas as complete theories. Bits and pieces of insight do not prove that these discoveries were made before Marx.


Mr. Avijit goes onto the favorite hobbyhorse of the bourgeoisie: The atrocities of Stalin.
First, one should read “Another View of Stalin” by Ludo Martins to clear ones mind of all the bourgeois propaganda against Stalin. http://www.plp.org/books/Stalin/book.html.
Second, even if you proved that Stalin committed atrocities, how does that disprove the materialist interpretation of history or the theory of surplus-value? From a strictly scientific point of view one has to disprove these theories do not accord to the objective world. If X made a mistake on Y occasion it has no bearing on whether A corresponds to B. Stalin’s real or alleged atrocities have no impact on the scientific validity of materialism or the theory of surplus-value. The fact is that you just couldn’t help taking the support of popular prejudice created from years of US propaganda to attempt to win your argument (i.e. proving that Marxism is not a science).


There is no need for me to answer all the slanderous accusations you have hurled against the communist leadership or the Soviet Union. Because frankly, none of this has any impact on the real debate that is going on: Is Marxism a science.
Last you assert that, “In my view, attraction of Marxist views among his followers is not because it is scientific but for the belief that western capitalist society is either inherently evil, or at least incapable of satisfying human needs.”


You are ascribing motives to people purely subjectively. I can tell you that I was attracted to Marxism because it is the most scientific explanation of history and critique of capitalism that I have read to date.


I hope that clarifies some misconceptions and inaccurate statements that you were ascribing to Marxism. I do not check this website regularly, therefore, if you write a response please notify me.


In solidarity
