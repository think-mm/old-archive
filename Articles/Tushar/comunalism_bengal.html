Socio-economic origins of communalism in Bengal

 Tushar Sarkar 

  

 

My main objective in trying to open a discussion on this subject in Mukto-mona is to explore the issue through ‘field work’; being a student and a professional in the earth sciences I have learnt the value of field work not only in that branch of science but also in social sciences. I am hoping that Mukto-mona will provide me with that rare opportunity of accessing the field where I can find fellow travellers from ‘both communities’.

My Hindu background, a social upbringing that is anti-Muslim and membership of the majority community in western Bengal are three big obstacles in becoming ‘objective’ in this subject. But, a continued and persistent hard battle to overcome these obstacles has enabled me to ‘seek truth from facts’ and through ‘re-education of intellectuals’.

I first tried to open the subject in Mukto-mona towards the end of February last year, but unfortunately, there was no response. This time I am very pleased to have three participations already. However, I am embarrassed to say that due to some unforeseen pressure of business, I won’t be able to participate fully in the debate and discussions until April/May. Meanwhile, I shall contribute in a very limited manner; the subject needs enormous research work.

S R Akhtar has opened a ‘Pandora’s box‘ for me! But, I think the problems are manageable. And, like Subimal, I also welcome his ‘bold’ approach, without necessarily agreeing with some of his assertions. Subimal has taken up and answered some of the issues, which I wanted to raise, but many of these are, in my opinion, of secondary importance at the present stage of the discussion. And I have very little expertise in the field of literature. I agree with Subimal that Akhtar is confusing "Being influenced by British culture" with "being influenced by the British liberal thinkers". Some say that Sukumar Roy’s famous and immensely enjoyable ‘Ha Ja Ba Ra La’ (I can still recite from memory large parts of it!!) was influenced by ‘Alice's adventures in wonderland’; but it would be ludicrous to call that ‘plagiarising’! Rabindranath was deeply influenced by Irish and Scottish music while he was in Britain, which led to much of his creative music. Let us leave Jibanananda and others out of this discussion for the moment, as I said these are matters of secondary importance just now.

I think, the only way to make progress in this discussion is to either give enough evidence for any viewpoint, or to admit that they are only assertions, hypotheses, being put forward for ‘testing’, to be backed by evidence later. On the basis of some initial studies on some aspects of the subject, I would like to put forward the following hypotheses, via quotes from relevant sources, without treating the quotes as evidence.

It is necessary to study the subject historically; (a) pre-1757, (b) 1757-1857, and (c) 1857-1905.

(a) Pre-1757 Hypothesis 1: During Mughal rule ‘Bengali nationalism’ was much stronger than communalism.

“….This is not to say that religious differences per se provide a satisfactory explanation for the course of modern Bengali politics, although it has often been interpreted in this way. The introduction of Islam has certainly had a marked effect upon Bengali polity, but the character of Islam in Bengal has not been quite the same as that in the northwestern part of India. Islam emerged as the dominant political force in India following the Turki-Afghan invasions of the twelfth century AD, establishing a firm base on the Indo-Gangetic plain during the rule of the Delhi Sultans.Bengal, on the eastern extremity of the Gangetic valley, received its introduction to Islam only after the religion's uncompromising orthodoxy had been mellowed by its experiences of Indian culture, the activities of the Sufis, and the infusion of converted Hindus into Islam's fold. As had been the case in other parts of India which came into contact with Islam, many of the converts came from the lower classes, attracted by the principles of equality and fraternity enshrined in the Koranic message. In Bengal, these converts not only retained their Bengali language, but much of their Hindu tradition as well. Others converted to Islam for economic advantage. In either case, the new Muslims were different in their approach to their faith from many of their more orthodox brethren in the west of India and beyond. Islam entered Bengal first not as a political force, but through the activities of Sufi mystics. The religious beliefs of these teachers were not out of keeping with indigenous Bengali philosophical tenets, and their mendicant behaviour was quite familiar to the majority of Bengalis, brought up in the presence of sadhus, Hindu holy men. On the philosophical level, there was much in common between Hinduism and liberal forms of Islam, but where proselytising took place amongst the lower classes in Bengal, philosophy was not of great importance, as was observed in a nineteenth century official report:

Some of them [the peasants of Bengal] have never heard of Mohammed; some regard him as a person corresponding in their system of thought to Rama or Lakshman of the Hindus. The Koran is hardly read even in Bengali, and in Arabic not at all; many of those who have heard of it cannot tell who wrote it. Yet any Moslem peasant is able to repeat a few scraps of prayer in Arabic.

Sufi and bhakti syncretism thus blurred the distinctions between forms of Hindu and Muslim worship in Bengal, but without achieving anything approaching a synthesis between the two religions……

Consciousness of Bengali identity pervading Hindu and Muslim society had an appreciable effect in the political sphere. Even during the great period of Mughal supremacy, Bengali 'nationalism' tended to compete with rather than complement the unifying character of Islam even among Bengali Muslims. Bengal, under the Mughals, was a rebellious state with a strong awareness of its regional identity, and its indigenous rulers took opportunities when they arose to exploit weaknesses in the central imperial authority”……..( Bangladesh and the Indian Ocean, 1971-1975, Denis Wright, Chapter 1, Origins of Bangladesh).

(b) 1757-1857 Hypothesis 2: It is understandable that the British would use the Hindus against the Muslim ruling classes from 1757. From 1757, the two Bengali communities, in eastern and western Bengal started to develop separately, under the leadership of respective landed gentry and upper middle classes.

“……Although it was not usually a matter of critical importance to the Bengali peasant who held the reins of power - indeed, rulers of both religious persuasions in India tended to make the same demands upon the peasantry - the Muslim community derived a measure of psychological security from the fact that Muslims were seen to be in the most vital positions of authority in government throughout the entire period of Muslim rule. Although for most of the Mughal period Bengali rather than Muslim nationalism was ascendant, the British assumption of political control forced Bengali Muslims to see their relationship with the Hindus in a new light. Without the psychological prop of Mughal dominance over India, Bengali Muslims gradually withdrew from the cooperation with Hindus which was essential for Bengali nationalism to retain its supremacy. The strength of Bengali nationalism steadily deteriorated during the British period in favour of antagonism between an ascendant class of prosperous Hindus and a mass of uneducated Muslim peasants and labourers…..

By several of its policies, the British administration exacerbated the growth of communalism in Bengal, although this result was, at first, unintentional. Under the Mughals, the Muslim aristocracy was an integral part of the economic system, passing on revenue collected by their Hindu agents to the emperor. With the British penetration of Bengal, the Muslim nobility lost this function, for the East India Company had no use for them in this capacity. Instead, the Hindu agents previously employed by the aristocracy became directly responsible to the Company. Aware of the potential for disorder which could follow if all avenues of administration were suddenly closed to the Muslim upper classes in Bengal, the Company allowed certain monopolies to remain in Muslim hands……..

In spite of these traditions, effective power soon passed from the hands of the Muslim nobility…….

The Bengali Muslim aristocracy had its own problems. It was more concerned with alleviating financial distress as a result of the implementation of Permanent Settlement than with organisations such as Sir Sayyid's. Introduced by Lord Cornwallis in 1793 and intended to modernise the old feudal structure, it proved unsuitable for prevailing conditions in Bengal, and taxed the old aristocracy virtually out of existence. The Muslim estate holders were forced to pay off their taxes by selling portions or all of their estates, and it was the rising class of Hindu banyans, with capital and knowledge of British law behind them, who bought up the land. As a direct result of Permanent Settlement, the last source of wealth for the Bengali Muslim aristocracy disappeared, and it fell into the hands of those who were already entrenched in the British administrative system. Permanent Settlement was also disastrous for the Bengali Muslim peasant, for 'by concentrating proprietary rights in the hands of the former state revenue collector (the zamindar) [it] wiped out at a stroke the peasant's hold on the land at the same time as transforming payments from a constant form of tax to rent which could legally be set at any level pleasing the landlord'.The peasant had no means of appeal against this system, and was at the mercy of his new landlords. ……Lord MacAuley's Minute recommending English as the medium of instruction for education was approved by Governor-General Bentinck on 7 March 1835, and in 1844 the government made it clear that western and English educated Indians would be preferred for public appointment.The result was a widening of the gap between Hindus and Muslims, especially as the growing Hindu upper class became identified by the Muslim peasantry as the class enemy. (emphasis added by TS).This sense of grievance exhibited by the Muslims extended to the British, and constituted a component of the Bengali Muslim role in the Mutiny of 1857. (Ibid)

“…The Hindus were in a better position in terms of economic status, professional qualities etc, than the Muslims. During the pre-Sepoy Mutiny period, section of Hindu traders greatly helped the British while their Muslim counterparts did not. The British were angry. With the spread of Western education, big way. But the Muslims could not.”(Subhayu Banerjee, Partition of Bengal, internet)

© 1857- 1905 Hypothesis 3: From 1857, the separate development of Hindu and Muslim communities solidified. By 1905 it was almost complete.

“The failure of the Mutiny had implications for Hindu-Muslim relations throughout India, and nowhere were its consequences more keenly felt than in Bengal. The British government bestowed the heaviest share of the blame for the uprising upon the Muslim community, whose dedication to the Mughal emperor (in whose name the 'mutiny' took place) was perceived to be the most fanatical. Following the suppression of the Mutiny, the British administration openly declared its intention to destroy any group which might persevere in attempts at armed opposition to the newly re-established British authority. This threat was aimed directly at Muslims, who were more prone to seek such a solution than the Hindus, having less to lose by doing so. While the Mutiny briefly opened up the possibility of winning freedom by revolutionary means, popular faith in these means was shaken by defeat. Even so, the suppression of the Mutiny did not eliminate entirely Muslim attempts to undermine British rule, and this in itself indicates the extent of Muslim frustration with British rule at the time. In the 1870s, the Wahhabi movement sent waves of agitation through Bengal, and the Bengali Muslim intelligentsia was under its influence. ( Bangladesh and the Indian Ocean, 1971-1975, Denis Wright, Chapter 1, Origins of Bangladesh).

“From 1870 onwards, the British started inciting the Hindus and the Muslims to form their own political parties to establish their distinct religious identities. That was perhaps, the beginning of the communalisation of politics. The British not only encouraged the two communities to form political parties along religious lines, they took various constructive steps to create a situation whereby Hindus and Muslims would be forced to think in a way as if their religious identity is at peril. This effort culminated in the partition of Bengal in 1905. West Bengal, Orissa and Bihar was on one side and the erstwhile east Bengal and Assam was on the other. The partition was made along communal lines. This partition provided an impetus to the religious divide and, as a result of that, All India Muslim League and All India Hindu Mahasabha was formed. Both the organisations aimed at fanning communal passions.” (Subhayu Banerjee, Partition of Bengal, internet)

“The publication of the original proposals towards the end of 1903 had aroused unprecedented opposition, especially among the influential educated middle-class Hindus. The proposed territorial adjustment seemed to touch the existing interest groups and consequently led to staunch opposition. The Calcutta lawyers apprehended that the creation of a new province would mean the establishment of a Court of Appeal at Dacca and diminish the importance of their own High Court. Journalists feared the appearance of local newspapers, which would restrict the circulation of the Calcutta Press. The business community of Calcutta visualised the shift of trade from Calcutta to Chittagong, which would be nearer, and logically the cheaper port. The Zamindars who owned vast landed estates both in west and east Bengal foresaw the necessity of maintaining separate establishments at Dhaka that would involve extra expenditure.

The educated Bengali Hindus felt that it was a deliberate blow inflicted by Curzon at the national consciousness and growing solidarity of the Bengali-speaking population. The Hindus of Bengal, who controlled most of Bengal's commerce and the different professions and led the rural society, opined that the Bengali nation would be divided, making them a minority in a province including the whole of Bihar and Orissa. They complained that it was a veiled attempt by Curzon to strangle the spirit of nationalism in Bengal. They strongly believed that it was the prime object of the government to encourage the growth of a Muslim power in eastern Bengal as a counterpoise to thwart the rapidly growing strength of the educated Hindu community. Economic, political and communal interests combined together to intensify the opposition against the partition measure…

The economic aspect of the (swadeshi) movement (1905) was partly responsible for encouraging separatist forces within the Muslim society. The superiority of the Hindus in the sphere of trade and industry alarmed the Muslims. Fear of socio-economic domination by the Hindus made them alert to safeguard their own interests. These apprehensions brought about a rift in Hindu-Muslims relations. In order to avoid economic exploitation by the Hindus, some wealthy Muslim entrepreneurs came forward to launch new commercial ventures. One good attempt was the founding of steamer companies operating between Chittagong and Rangoon in 1906………..

In the context of the partition the pattern of the land system in Bengal played a major role to influence the Muslim mind. The absentee Hindu zamindars made no attempt to improve the lot of the raiyats who were mostly Muslims. The agrarian disputes (between landlords and tenants) already in existence in the province also appeared to take a communal colour. It was alleged that the Hindu landlords had been attempting to enforce Swadeshi ideas on the tenants and induce them to join the anti-partition movement” (‘First Partition of Bengal’, source unknown, internet)

Summing up:

“…the result was the creation of a Hindu middle class without a significant Muslim counterpart, with Hindu political and economic power centred upon Calcutta. The mass of the peasantry, which constituted the most vulnerable class in Bengali society, was overwhelmingly Muslim. A very small aristocratic Muslim elite existed in British Bengal as a legacy of medieval Muslim empires, but this elite and the peasantry had nothing in common.” Bangladesh and the Indian Ocean, 1971-1975, Denis Wright, Chapter 1, Origins of Bangladesh).

The socio-economic origins of communalism in Bengal are to be found in the rise of Hindu and Muslim merchant classes and upper middle classes, ‘created by the bureaucratic and industrial imperialism of the British’.

The development of the democratic revolution in Bengal was thwarted by British colonialism, using communalism as the main tool. For understandable reasons, anti-imperialist nationalism developed more amongst the Hindu middle classes than amongst the Muslim counterparts.
