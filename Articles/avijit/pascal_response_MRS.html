let me give you some examples to understand how expectation value works. Suppose in a casino in Genting Island  you have the option of paying a dollar to play a game in which there is an equal chance of returning nothing, and returning three dollars. The expectation of the game itself is

0*(1/2) + 3*(1/2) = 1.5,
so the expectation of paying a dollar (-1)  for certain, then playing, is
-1 + 1.5 = 0.5.
This exceeds the expectation of not playing (namely 0), so you should play.

On the other hand, if the game gave an equal chance of returning nothing, and returning two dollars, then its expectation would be:

0*(1/2) + 2*(1/2) = 1.
Then consistent with decision theory, you could either pay the dollar to play, or refuse to play, for either way your overall expectation would be -1+1 = 0.

Pascal considered f as a heavenly reward = infinity.

  God exists God does not exist
Wager for God ∞ f1 (finite value)
Wager against God f2 (finitevalue) f3 (finite value)

 

E(wager for God) = ∞*p + f1*(1 − p) = ∞

That is, your expected utility of belief in God is infinite — as Pascal puts it, "our proposition is of infinite force".

On the other hand, your expected utility of wagering against God is

E(wager against God) = f2*p + f3*(1 − p)

This is finite. Rationality requires you to perform the act of maximum expected utility. Therefore, rationality requires you to wager for God. This is what Pascal concluded.
