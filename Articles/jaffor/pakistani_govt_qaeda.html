Pakistani government paid Waziri tribal chiefs the money they owed to al-Qaeda

 A.H. Jaffor Ullah


When Alexander the Great told his General, Seleucus, that what a strange land India was, he was standing somewhere in Punjab or Northwest Frontier province (NWFP). In the second week of February 2005, I read several stories in the Internet about disbursement of large sum of money to several tribal chiefs in South Waziristan by Pakistani government. After reading the news, I also felt the same way. Indeed, Balochistan where Waziristan is located is a strange place. After reading this commentary I am confident that many readers would concur with me that the tribal land, which is located northeast of Balochistan and southwest of NWFP, is a bit strange place.


In 2004, under pressure from Bush Administration General Musharraf ordered his army to comb South Waziristan for al-Qaeda leaders and soldiers who were in hiding in that mountainous region of Pakistan. Rumors were floating in Pakistan that in the aftermath of America’s invasion of Afghanistan in November-December 2001, many al-Qaeda soldiers and their leaders had fled to neighboring Balochistan and NWFP to save their heads. For one thing, there was no dearth of local sympathizers of Talibans in that region, which is also known as ‘tribal areas.’ In all likelihood, the local tribal people have given a safe haven to al-Qaeda soldiers. Now they admit it quite openly.


When the Pakistani soldiers attacked the mountainous village in South Waziristan near Wana looking for al-Qaeda militants, they knew right away that they were in hostile territories. Snipers were allover the place in villages dotting Pakistan-Afghan border. The tribal people were sympathetic to al-Qaeda causes; they did not mind taking side while firing and killing Pakistani soldiers. The news of this army casualty was flashed in many newspapers’ front page in the Internet in the last week of September 2004.


Pakistani military wanted to appease the tribal elders because they know that the terrain is difficult over there to fight a conventional war. The fight between the military and Waziri militants took a breather in later part of 2004 for we did not hear the news of more casualties in South Waziristan. Now it is becoming very clear that Pakistani military was in negotiation with the tribal leaders so that they would not hide al-Qaeda militants in the mountain hideouts and not fire at Pakistani military. The news that come out on February 9, 2005 makes it very clear what did happen in the past few months of intense negotiations between the military and the tribal chiefs in South Waziristan.


Another piece of news that hit the Internet recently, also attracted my attention. Of all places on earth, the news came from South Waziristan. On February 7, 2005, gunmen killed two Pakistani journalists and wounded another one in the troubled tribal region. The journalists were heading back from an official ceremony held to mark a peace deal between the government and a tribal militant leader when assailants riding in a car opened fire on their vehicle in Wana, the main town of South Waziristan, which is near Afghan border.
The ceremony was held underneath a tent in Sararogoha, which is located about 80 kilometers northeast of Wana. Based on month-long negotiation, the Pakistani government granted amnesty to a Waziristan renegade Baitullah Mehsud and about 70 of his fighters in return for a pledge not to harbor foreign militants (read al-Qaeda militants). An emotional tribal elder, Maulana Saleh Shah said, “This Pakistan, this Waziristan, this soil belongs to me, I belong to this soil. These Mujahids belong to me, I belong to them; this Pakistani army belongs to me, I belong to this army.” Another tribal leader, Malik Jahangir Khan attending the ceremony said, “Suddenly, somehow, the situation had deteriorated. But sometimes relations can become strained even between father and son. Today, by the grace of Allah, all those brothers who were annoyed with one another, who had some grievances among them, are assembled on one platform, are sitting together.”


At the ceremony, the tribal elders, armed fighters and government officials shook hands and offered prayers for the success of the truce, as armed guards of the militants requested media not to take any pictures.


The wave of attacks in Wana, South Waziristan, in September 2004 came as the Pakistani government was trying to quell growing assaults by the tribal militants fighting for more autonomy in the neighboring southwestern province of Balochistan, although many analysts say there was no evidence of links between the two causes, viz., fight for autonomy and giving support to fleeing al-Qaeda militants.


Defying the request of tribesmen, the Pakistani reporters took some photos some of which showed up in the Yahoo news site and that irked some tribal leaders. As the reporters were returning from South Waziristan, shots were fired at them. Hospital sources said two journalists died instantly while another was seriously wounded. While no one has claimed responsibility for the attack, it is widely believed that tribal militants who had abetted al-Qaeda militants some of them may have killed the journalists.


On February 10, 2005, I read in the Internet that the Pakistan government paid huge amounts of money to four of the five most-wanted militants in South Waziristan, who surrendered and signed peace deals with the authorities in November 2004. The money was paid to them so that they could repay the debt they claimed they owed to Al Qaeda. Pakistan’s premier newspaper ‘Dawn’ has learnt about the payment to militant tribal leaders by the military.
The Pakistani government wanted to appease the militant tribal leaders; therefore, payments were made as part of a package after the militants insisted that they needed the money to pay back a huge sum they had borrowed or taken from Al Qaeda in their fight against Pakistani forces. This is quite a revelation!


According to the Dawn, the sources said the government paid four tribal leaders from the secret service fund (SS Fund) – a kind of slush fund - and the four militant commanders were brought to Peshawar for signing the peace document in November 2004.


Two of them, Haji Sharif and Maulvi Abbas, received Rupees 15 million each, while Maulvi Javed Karmazkhel and Haji Mohammad Omar were each paid Rupees 1 million. Maulvi Abdul Aziz, the fifth militant leader, who also signed the peace deal, was not part of the package and, therefore, did not get any amount. However, the sources said that Maulvi Aziz was now very upset and angry for being ignored and was reportedly pressing the other four militants to give him his share. The payments to the Ahmadzai-Wazir tribal militants were made in January 2005, while Haji Sharif received his share on February 4, 2005, the sources said.
The story dealing with the disbursement of bribe money to Waziri tribal leaders that just came out is very intriguing, to say the least. The tribal leaders are opportunist and they could be easily bought with money. It very well could be that they made up the story about their debt to al-Qaeda. The Pakistani military paying a handful of tribal leaders to win their heart is both intriguing and troubling. America is now in the middle of fighting a global terrorism. Thus, they could easily pay the Pakistani government money to win the heart of tribal leaders. It remains to be seen if the hush money paid to 4-5 tribal leaders in South Waziristan by Pakistani military would bring the expected result, i.e., withdrawing support to the al-Qaeda militants who are in shambles in the wake of American invasion in November 2001.


In summary, the news of two Pakistani journalists killed in Wana, South Waziristan, in the hands of tribesmen brought another piece of news that startled many people in Pakistan. It turns out that Pakistani military is now paying bribes to Waziri tribal leaders to win their heart in an effort to catch the remnants of al-Qaeda militants in Pakistan-Afghan border are near northwest Balochistan.


This is the strategy of the Bush Administration and Pakistani government headed by General Pervez Musharraf is an eager partner in this venture to wipe out the remaining vestiges of al-Qaeda in that part of the troubled world. If history is any guide, when the bribe money will all vanish, the tribesmen will again side with al-Qaeda remnants to extract more money from Pakistani government. The tribal leaders in that part of the world are unprincipled and the Musharraf Administration knows it very well. However, it is politically expedient to quell the uprise in South Waziristan. Musharraf government is exactly doing that.
