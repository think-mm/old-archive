Obituary Note

 

A.H. Salahuddin, an eminent film producer and director (1924-2003)

 

 Bangladesh’s renowned film producer and director A.H. Salahuddin passed away on October 26, 2003 in a nursing home in Valparaiso, Indiana, USA.  Mr. Salahuddin had suffered brain hemorrhage in January 1998 and was comatose for the last five years and nine months.  He was 79 at the time of his death.  His wife Mrs. Ruhia Salahuddin was present at the time of his demise.  He is survived by his only daughter Dr. Sigma Salahuddin of Indianapolis, USA, four sisters, Amina Khatun (Sweden), Nazma Khatun, Saleha Khatun, and Zobeda Khatun, all living in Dhaka, and two brothers, Dr. A.K. Jalaluddin (New Delhi), and Shahabuddin (Chicago, USA).

 

Even though Mr. Salahuddin was known for his legendary folk movie, Roopban, which he made in the mid 1960s, he was one of the few creative directors who made earlier such classic movies of Bangladesh as “Je Nadi Morupothe” (his first movie made in early 1960s), “Dharapat,” “Surjasnan,” and “Tero Number Fekuostagar Lane.”  He was among a handful of directors in Dhaka whose work paved the way for rapid growth in Bangladesh’s film industry.  Many would be directors who had worked under him throughout 1960s were influenced by his cinematic techniques.  He was a very likable person.  He was also the chairman of Bangladesh’s only gramophone record company, which he started in late 1960s.  He started his career as a demonstrator in science at Victoria College, Comilla, in the early 1950s after finishing his bachelor degree in science in Kolkata.  Staging many dramas in Comilla in the 1940s and 1950s gave him ideas to move into celluloid world.  Many cinemagoers of Bangladesh from yesteryears know him as a film director who made movies based on real life.  Later he switched gear to make a folk story-based movie, “Roopban,” which was an instant hit.  This movie made a big splash throughout erstwhile East Pakistan and especially in the rural areas.  The movie ushered in a new genre of film in Bangladesh.  Mr. Salahuddin left Dhaka for London in disguise during 1971 Liberation War because the Pakistani military was hunting him down for publishing Sheikh Mujib’s famous speech given at the Ramna Racecourse on March 7, 1971. 

 

Mr. Salahuddin immigrated to America in 1992 where he settled in Bloomington, Indiana.  He primarily came to America for his daughter’s education.  He was leading a retired life in abroad while visiting family members and friends.  He was maternal uncle to Jamal Hasan, a well-known cyber writer in various Bangladeshi forums in the Internet.

 

Mr. Salahuddin hails from Brahmanbaria.  His father, Moulvi Abdul Latif was a Deputy Magistrate during the British Raj.  His wife, Mrs. Ruhia Salahuddin, was a school headmistress in Dhaka during 1980s and 1990s.  With the passing of Mr. Salahuddin Bangladesh lost a pioneer film producer and director.
