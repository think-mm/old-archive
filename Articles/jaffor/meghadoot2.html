First four poems
in Bangla (Bengali) and English
translated from the original Sanskrti

By A.H. Jaffor Ullah

Kali Dasa, a North Indian Sanskrit poet, wrote these poems perhaps in fifth or sixth century A.D. Sri Narendra Dev did the translation of these poems into Bangla in 1920s. The first edition of his compilation was published in Kolkata (Calcutta) in 1930. He did the translation twice. Here I present the translated poems from the book publisshed in the first edition, which many connoisseurs think is a better translation than the second set of translated poems. For one thing, each and every poem follows closely the Sanskrit original. The second set of translation was probably done in 1950s.

Here is tidbit of information about Narendra Deb's only daughter, which might interest the readers. Sri Narendra Dev and his wife (Radharani Debi) had only one child. She is none other than Srimati Nabaneeta Dev Sen, a writer by her own right. She was born in 1938. Later, she married Prof. Amartya Sen under an unusual circumstance. In an interview she said, 'I was christened Nabaneeta by Tagore.' To readers outside her native West Bengal, Nabaneeta Deb Sen needs a little more introductions. She had to come back from Cambridge halfway through her doctoral studies to get married to Amartya Sen, the Nobel Laureate. She has been teaching Comparative Literature at Jadavpur University for the past 27 years. Her marriage to Prof. Sen did not last, though. Well, Ms. Nabanita Deb Sen and Prof. Amartya Sen are the product of mid twentieth century when divorce is no longer considered a taboo among educated class in Bengal anymore.

One more bit of information about Nabanita Deb Sen might interest you. She has two daughters; the eldest, Antara, is a journalist and her second daughter, Nandana Deb Sen, had acted as heroine in a foreign movie "Seducing Maarya" in 1999. One newspaper report described her as follows: "Tall, all of 5'7?, lissome with a waistline which will make any teenager blush, there's a certain childlike innocence in her delicate pale face, in spite of her finely chiseled cheekbones." Nandana is a prolific scriptwriter as well.

I think I have written enough about Narendra Deb and his descendants.

Some words on clouds' photo that are gracing the web page you are browsing now. In July 2001, I took photos of clouds from a commercial jetliner on my way to New Orleans (my hometown) from Philadelphia. I never thought at the time I was taking these pictures that I will be able to use them for any worthwhile purposes. But now, they came very handy. It was a daunting task to overlay the poems in Bangla script over the photo. After repeated trials, I was able to do it. Nonetheless, it was very time consuming. All my efforts will be deemed worthwhile if readers can appreciate reading these gems in the Internet. I am now translating these poems into English keeping the original flavor. Professor Joanna Kirkpatrick is helping me out to polish my first batch of translation. More poems will be available as I make progress in translating Kali Dasa's immortal poems from the original Sanskrit.

Enjoy these fine poems.

The English translation was all done by me (A.H. Jaffor Ullah)

          A lean and lovesick Jakkha dallied over his work
          And made his elder quite angry;
          He was thus sent away for a long time.
          Now he is lovelorn, nothing helps
          His misery knows no bounds.
          He settled down in Ramgiri Parvat near a crystal lake,
          whose cool water once touched Sita,
          And there he lives in desolation.
          Frustrated and forlorn,
          Distant Chitrakut Mountain his new home,
          Jakkha is lost in desolation and starved for love,
          So thin his lean wrist drops its golden jewels.
          It's the first of Ashar. Filling the southern sky
          A cloud, frolicksome as an elephant about to charge,
          Seems to lower his tusks.
      That cloud is rising high on the mountaintop -- what a pretty sight.
      Filled with desire and tearful, Jakkha
      Suppressed his feelings and plunged into deep reflection.
      O Cloud! You have the power to sway my heart.
      Look at me -- How I pine to caress the long neck of my beloved.
      Should my feelings not be moved?
      What's so strange about that?


        The monsoon's first month has passed
        And Sravana approaches.
        Jakkha thinks his beloved must be pining away.
        He needs to revive her with news of his well-being,
        Tell her he is still alive.
        The towering monsoon clouds invite his gaze.
        With fresh and rain-drenched Kutoj flowers everywhere
        He will string a fresh garland and,
        With entreating words, ask the cloud
        To be the messenger of his lovesick poems.
        O cloud, I hail you with folded hands.
