Kali Dasa's
The tales of Jakkha and flight path of Megh

 Some background information

By A.H. Jaffor Ullah

Considering the subject matter of Kali Dasa's 'Meghadoot,' I should have taken an interest in it a long time ago, but for whatever reasons I did not. Now I am immensely interested the masterpiece collection of poems by this legendary poet of North India. He penned his poems in Sanskrit, which was a living language then. Some may consider his poems from Meghadoot laced with eroticism. Nonetheless, these poems are very narrative (descriptive) and at the same time very expressive in the sense that the poems outpour human emotions. For example, many a poem reflects the feelings of a tormented soul - a young Jakkha, inhabitant of Aloka, a Himalayan town north of modern-day Almora in UP, India. According to legend, Aloka is located in High Himalaya next to Manos Saravar (Lake Manos), the place where from the Ganges originate.

There are many legends surrounding Jakkhas of ancient India. The members of Jakkha clan are also known as Kuber. Among myriad legends about Jakkha, first there is this one -- they were extremely rich. Who has not heard of Jakkha's Dhon or Wealth? In Sanskrit, Kuber means Ku (Ugly or bad) + ber (body) or unsightly face. According to legend, Kubers are three-legged humans and they have only eight teeth, their right eye burned or hazel eyed. From Ramayana, we learn that they lived originally in Lanka-the modern day Sri Lanka. The original Kuber is the son of Bisraba and Debobarnini and his name was Boisraban. He was the treasurer of gods and goddesses. He was stepbrother to Ravana, the famous villain of Ramayana whole stole Rama?s wife, Sita. Because of war between Rama and Ravana, Kuber left the island and moved deep into the mainland. He settled down in a place in high Himalaya. His descendents lived in Aloka or Alokadham, the most beautiful and rich city in ancient India. Aloka's fabled women were renowned for their physical beauty and charm. The beauty of the gardens of that fabled town also drew attention of many poets.

The narrator of Meghadoot is a young Kuber who is introduced as Jakkha. According to legend, the young Jakkha was appointed a guardsman in one of the many pretty parks in Aloka. He was recently married to a gorgeous young girl. He could not resist the temptation of visiting his lover in the daytime to be with her when he was supposed to guard the garden. He ignored his duty to be united physically with his newly married wife. In his absence, according to a legend, an elephant trampled some flowerbeds. The news was leaked out to Jakkha's supervisor. The elder then decided to send young Jakkha alone to Ramgiri Parvat (modern day Ramtek, north of Nagpur) located about 650 miles south of Aloka to live there for a year.

Jakkha found a place to live in a hermitage in Ramgiri Parvat. There was lake next to the hermitage. According to legend, Sita also bathed in this lake making its water holy. At the advent of monsoon season, Jakkha saw towering cloud of cumulus 'castellatus' (castle-like rain producing clouds) coming from the south and moving northwardly towards Aloka where his beloved lives. An idea evolved in lovelorn Jakkha's mind seeing these magnificent cumulus rain clouds. He badly wanted to send his Sandeshas or Kusholbarta (news) to his beloved. Thus, Megh (cloud) becomes his messenger. He approaches the cloud with reverence. He greets him with affectionate words and then tells the cloud how to get to Aloka from Ramgiri. All these descriptions are there in Kali Dasa's semi-erotic poems. He also tells the cloud what to say when it will reach his beloved. The entire 117 poems describe in vivid detail the landscape, lore of the land, love ballad, longing, and other human qualities of a desolate life that Jakkha was leading. In the last line of Meghadootam, Jakkha warns the Cloud not to separate from lightning in the long journey that lies ahead because lovelorn Jakkha knows the pain of separation from one's beloved. Everyone needs a lover even the thunder-producing cumulus cloud has 'Lightning' as its consort just as Shiva has Parvati. Thus, we see that even Kali Dasa brings the Eastern concept of Yinyang - the two complementary forces or principles that encompasses all aspects or phenomena of life -- into Meghadootam.

The Cloud Messenger's whimsical flight path

When the lovelorn Jakkha implored the Could Messenger to deliver his messages to his beloved in Aloka, he gave a detail flight path to the messenger. The Cloud could have gone straight north from Ramgiri Parvat (modern day Ramtek) to Aloka-dham (abode). Jakkha, however, gave the Cloud Messenger a very complicated flight path. Sometime going to the north, sometime veering to the east and sometime going northeast. He made sure that the Cloud would go over villages and town so that people living there could see him. He also knew that without monsoon rain there could be no agriculture. The entire 64 poems included in the Purba-Megh (Pre-Megh) part of the poem collection describe in vivid detail the itinerant journey of Cloud Messenger from Ramgiri to Aloka in high Himalaya. Some of the advice to Cloud Messenger is utterly funny. In one place, Jakkha tells the cloud to scare the villagers by pelting hails. There are ample descriptions of Amrakoot Mountain in poem number seventeen, eighteen, and nineteen (Purba-megh); similarly, in poem number twenty five the ancient poet describes the nation of Bidisha and its capital Dosharno; there is even the mention of turbulent Bethrabati River. In poem number twenty-eight and twenty-nine (Purbo-megh), we notice the mention of ancient cities such as Ujjayini, Shoudhapur, and Nirvindya River. In poems thirty-one through thirty-three (Purbo-megh), Kali Dasa describes meticulously not only the art of applying makeup by the pretty maidens of fabled town Avanti, but also how they both perfumized and color their long and wavy hair. There is also the mention of one Shipra River whose cool waters refreshed all the pretty maidens who spent the night enveloped by their lovers' arm. The Cloud Messenger should not forget to visit Devagiri on his way to Aloka to pay homage to Shiva and Parvati. Then he will cross the town of Dashapura on the Bank of Sindhu River. After that, he will cross the Mahabharata-famed battleground Kurukhetra to reach the ancient land of Kon-Khol. The Cloud Messenger will surely cross the land where sacred waters of Jahannobi-Jamuna met. His flight path will then take him to mountainous land where one encounters evergreen deodar forest (devadaru in Sanskrit) before he reaches many kingdoms in High Himalaya. There, he will first encounter the Kroncho Mountain before reaching Mount Kailas and then Manos Lake. The Cloud Messenger should quench his thirst by drinking the holy water of Manos Sarabar (lake) before resuming his journey on the way to Aloka, his final destination.

An assumed flight path* of Cloud Messenger from Ramgiri to Aloka
From the above description of Cloud Messenger's journey one could imagine that it was not a straightforward trip from south to north. In his meandering journey, the Cloud Messenger covered many a fabled towns. In the map provided here I dotted the line to show the flight path of the entire trip. The names of many ancient towns are placed on the map for easy identification. These towns are essentially located in modern-day Madhya Pradesh and Uttar Pradesh. Kali Dasa composed about 64 poems to describe the itinerant journey of the Cloud Messenger. The rest -- about 54 poems -- were devoted to Sandesha (messages) for Jakkha's beloved wife. Those poems describe in vivid detail the description of Aloka, the Himalayan town famed its for natural beauty, riches, pretty women, and buildings. These poems also describe in detail the beauty of unification between lovers. Jakkha serves one purpose via sending his Sandeshas or Bartas (messages) to his beloved to bide at home, until he returns from Ramgiri Parvat.
