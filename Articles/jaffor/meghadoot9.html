Purbo-Megh
Poem 29 through 32

Bangla translation by: Narendra Deb (done in 1930s)
English translation by: A.H. Jaffor Ullah and Joanna Kirkpatrick

 

Poem 29
              The Nibindha river is an untamed woman.
              Her jeweled waist ornaments, like tumbled stones,
              Impede the gushing stream. Look!
              In the eddy you can see her navel.
              Embrace her as she imparts her virginal whispers.

Poem 30
            Aren't you lucky, my friend--you find the
            Rivermaiden in a weakened condition! Look at these fallen
            Leaves, strewn like unbound hair in the current.
            But she is happy at your approach, so
            Do what is necessary to renew her.

 


Poem 31
          Visit Bishala city in Abantiland, as it waits calmly for you.
          Ancient sages taught the elders secrets for
          Protecting its beauty. Although it resembles a divine,
          Rock-like biscuit nibbled by the Swarga dwellers
          And then discarded -- like a gift from heaven
          It now resides here, thanks to the Abantians' virtues.

Poem 32
            Fresh breezes from River Sipra wash over Bishala town.
            Long-necked storks call their morning mantras,
            Beautiful but unknown. As the wind spreads their songs,
            The lotuses in full bloom waft fragrance on the same wind,
            Like scents spoured on lovers' bodies to revive them.


            Discrepancy between our and Narendra Deb's translation of Meghadoot
