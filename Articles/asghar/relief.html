Relief for Bangladesh

By Mohammad Asghar

Dear Moderator,
 
Mukto-mona has issued an appeal for donations for the flood victims of Bangladesh. I agree with your appeal, and also support it. But having myself once been involved in the distribution of relief, I know how it works in Bangladesh. Relief in Bangladesh are generally used to fatten the fat cats; to advance political careers and to elevate social status. If not most but a large portion of the relief materials that come from abroad does not reach the really effected people. If not all but the larger part of the money is pocketed by politicians, local leaders and the goons they nurture for their own benefits.
 
You can draw an inference from the following press report that appeared in New Age today. You may pay special attention to the highlighted portion of the reoprt, which reads as under:

 

 
Little food, that too stale, complain flood victims
Doctors allegedly ignore some city shelters

Friday July 30 2004 12:39:01 PM BDT

MOAZZEM HOSSAIN and HELEMUL ALAM


"People at the flood centres of the city alleged the relief workers deprived them of baby food and nutritious meals although some philanthropic organisations sent those to the centres for the victims.

Despite the announcement of the Dhaka City Corporation authorities that relief work had begun four days ago, there are flood centres in the city where relief materials, particularly oral saline and water-purifying tablets, are yet to be reached. People at the flood centres complained that the amount of food they had received until now was inadequate.

A source in the control room of the Dhaka City Corporation told New Age on Thursday that they had distributed a total of 14,600 packets of food, 60,635 packets of oral saline and 44,775 water-purifying tablets to 2.54 lakh people in 225 centres of the affected 58 city wards till Thursday.

They eat the delicious foods and we get the low quality foods , said an angry Amena Begum at the Kabi Nazrul Govt College centre in the old part of the city on Thursday noon. A bowl of kedgeree she received as relief smelled stale.

The food tastes bitter and my baby does not want to eat it, said Amena, mother of a four-year-old child.

Mayur Pankhi at the same centre said that the packets of baby food like milk, flattened rice and biriyani were brought to the centre on Wednesday noon. But they were immediately removed for reasons unknown to us.  When asked, a policeman at the Mahanagar Mohila College told New Age that he had seen baby food like dried milk and some rich food like biriyani and flattened rice brought by a social organisation on Wednesday noon, but he did not know whether they were distributed.

The flood-victims at centres including Dhaka Mahanagar Mohila College and Dhaka Govt Muslim High School said they had not been provided with rooms inside the centre. The doors of the building remained locked and hundreds of the victims had to take shelter in the veranda or in the open ground of the institutions. “We are soaked if there is rain and we are burned if there is sun,” said Mobarak Miah who has come from Keraniganj with his wife and four children as his thatched house had gone under water last week.

With six children to feed, Hanufa Begum felt helpless as she was given only a bowl of kedgeree, a meal hardly enough for two.

They give the same amount of food without considering the number of family members, said Hanufa on Thursday noon at the Kabi Nazrul College centre.

Md Rustam Sheikh at the Dhaka Govt Muslim High School was slapped on Wednesday noon as he insistently wanted another piece of bread for his younger child. Many at the same centre alleged that they were ill-treated on Wednesday noon when Dhaka Mayor Sadeque Hossain Khoka visited the centre.

We were told to get lost after standing in a queue for two hours as the mayor was scheduled to come, said Arman Miah. “We were asked to make a queue again at 2:00pm and then the mayor came at around 3:00pm.

Md Nazimuddin, secretary of Aunirban Sangsad, a social organisation that is carrying out relief work at the Dhaka Govt Muslim High School, said they are facing trouble in managing about 1,500 people who had taken shelter in the centre as they had received nothing from the city corporation.

Although there were policemen to ensure security in the centre, no physicians were found on duty. Nazimuddin said a doctor or any medical personnel is yet to come to the centre with a supply of medicine including oral saline and water-purifying tablets.

People at the flood centre have to fetch water from the Bahadur Shah Park as the authorities have kept the school bathrooms locked, they said.

A couple bought saline from a nearby shop for their two babies, Ayna and Marium, who were infected by diarrhoea on Thursday morning. We requested the relief workers for some saline but all they did was to show us the medicine shop,  said Rahima Khatun who had to look alone after her babies as her husband had gone to pull a rickshaw." (The New Age)

Before handing out our hard-earned money to unknown persons or organizations, we must ensure that what we are giving them would reach the flood victims. We must also ensure that the distributors of the relief do not squander most of it for their personal purposes.
 
I do not know how Mukto-mona might take the above measures, but I can assure you that if you should fail to take the suggested measures, in that event, most of your good and humanitarian efforts would go in vain.
 
Regards,
Mohammad Asghar
