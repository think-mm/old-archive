Mujib-Zia: Distortion of History

 By Mohammad Asghar

I was delighted to read Mr. Mohammad Ahsan’s write up in Mukto-mona, through which he tried to put the pre-independence history of Bangladesh in its right and true perspective. Like many other Bangladeshis, who had heard Sk. Mujib speak on that historical day of March 7, 1970, I believe that the words he had used in his that speech amounted to declaring independence for Bangladesh and that he did have to issue further declaration, oral or written, for motivating the people of East Pakistan into waging their struggle against the armed forces of Pakistan. 

Readers can read the article at: Mujib-Zia. Distortion of History  ( www. mukto-mona.com )

 I have made the above observation not because I am a follower of Awami League, or that I have an agenda of my own to advance through it. Nor am I trying to glorify the personality of Sk. Mujib unnecessarily, for I do not possess a sycophantic mentality. Despite being a critic of his administrative shortfalls, I respected him like my father. I always believed that had he not motivated the Bengalees to fight for the independence of Bangladesh, I would not have become in my life what I am today. The independence of Bangladesh helped me in becoming a businessman; it enabled me to go around the world a number of times and also, it gave me everything that I have today, be it a good life or the respect and honor I had earned during my business career in Bangladesh. 

Had not Bangladesh become an independent country, I am pretty sure of what I would have become: living under the domain of the West Pakistanis, I would, perhaps, have become a General Manager of a Bank after serving it until the end of my life, and that, too, if I had been successful in keeping my Pakistani bosses pleased during the entire length of my service.

 But the creation of Bangladesh changed my life; I became a businessman, I set up a bank and an insurance company with some friends, and had a wonderful life for so long as I and my family lived in the land of my birth. 

My gratitude to Sk. Mujib did not prevent me from criticizing him on his face, and this chance came to me in 1974, when I had the opportunity of seeing him at his Dhanmondi residence in presence of his younger brother, Sk. Nasir. 

Readers might recollect that in 1974, Bangladesh had faced its first serious food crisis. To help us out, Japan had agreed to donate 93,000 metric tons of rice, with the condition that Bangladesh would have to arrange its transportation at its own cost. My company was involved for shipping and transporting the entire quantity of rice to Chittagong.

 Phuni Bhushan Majumder was the Food Minister. For reasons unknown to me, he was delaying in the opening of the Letter of Credit for the freight. Having failed in all my efforts, I enlisted Sk. Nasir’s help whom I knew from my days in Khulna. As a good friend, he came down to Dhaka and asked me to see him at Sheikh’s Dhanmondi residence in the early morning of a day. 

I reached Dhanmondi on schedule, and was taken immediately to the Sheikh’s private study on the second floor of the building. While Nasir and I were hardly seated, the Sheikh arrived, wearing a lungi and a ganji (vest), with his tobacco pipe in his hand. He sat down in front of me, and wanted to know what my problem was. I told him about the Letter of Credit and the displeasure the Japanese were having due to the delay in its opening.

He picked up his green phone; asked his Secretary to connect him to “Phuni,” and when he was connected, he asked him whether or not he was having any problem with the opening of the Letter of Credit. I did not hear what Phuni was saying, but I still remember what the Sheikh had told him: Phuni, you know we are in the midst of a serious food crisis, and yet, you are sitting on the Letter of Credit. I want you to open it in two hours’ time, and then let me have a copy of it before noon today.  

He then turned to me. Which political party do you belong to? He asked me. I said: Sir, I belong to only one party and that is my stomach. He looked at my face quizzically and then said: We are going to hold elections soon. Are you going to vote for my party?

 I said: Sir, I respect you like my father. But I am also one of your critics. I do not like the way you have been running the administration; it is in a shambles. Take the example of the matter you have just helped me out of. Why should I have come and wasted your valuable time for such a legitimate, but trifling matter? I would prefer not to have you as a prime minister of the country, but as a Father of the Nation, and a true guide. Therefore, I am not going to vote for Awami League in the next or any other elections? While I was confronting the Sheikh, Nasir remained mum, with a sense of extreme uneasiness. But I was confident, for he was with me...

 The Sheikh gave me a hard look; stood up, came behind my chair and threw a punch on my back.  Before I could know what was going on, he said: Get out from here right now.  Come back at noon to pick up the copy of the Letter of Credit. Before I could say, thank you, sir, he was gone.

 I hold Sk. Mujib in the same esteem today as the one I had for him in 1974. Is it wrong in any way? Why people can’t give him the place he deserves in the history of Bangladesh? Who are these people who have been creating unnecessary confusion in the minds of the innocent people of Bangladesh?

 I believe that the number of people who have been creating confusion about Sk. Mujib is very small, and that most of them are either politicians or intellectuals. They have been doing it to serve their own political and intellectual purposes, and they would be continuing to do so for so long as it would be necessary for them. But the question is: how concerned are the ordinary people of the country on the question of who had, or who had not declared the independence of Bangladesh in 1970?

 In my understanding, the bulk of the Bangladeshi people do neither have any time nor any energy to care about the dispute; they are too busy with the struggles of their own lives. For a day laborer or a rickshaw puller, it is a non-issue. But not for the politicians, as they thrive on controversies, and for whom the wellbeing of the poor people have no meaning or significance. It is an important matter for some of us, who have their breakfast, lunch and dinner served on dinning tables; eat sumptuous meals, and hire helicopters to attend marriage ceremonies. It is important for those of us who consider themselves to be educated and, enlightened, and thus the guardians of the nation’s fate and future!

 I want to close this segment of my write up by asking: Is the question on the declaration of independence for Bangladesh an issue that needs to be debated and argued upon, even after thirty-three years of its existence?

 In connection with the war of liberation, I have a question for all, who may read this write up, to respond:

 The very day Sk. Mujib arrived in Bangladesh, he announced that the Pakistanis had killed three million people of Bangladesh during their nine months’ struggle for independence. My question is: how the Sheikh was able to arrive at this number, just days after the independence of the country, without carrying out any survey or investigation? Could not the number of the dead have been, say, four million, or just a couple of hundreds of thousand?

 I have had an opportunity to exchange views with Mr. Ahsan on another topic through Mukto-mona some days ago. From his argument I concluded that he is well versed with Islam and that he is also fully acquainted with all the doctrines and practices of his religion. Having this belief in my mind, I want to ask him, and other Muslims through him, a few questions, which follow as under:

 Point 1: On the preservation of martyrs’ skull, Mr. Ahsan wrote: “About the skulls, let peace be upon the martyrs and May Allah bless them with Jannah for their sacrifices.”

 Question: In the struggle for independence for Bangladeshi, many Hindus also had laid down their lives. Would they also enter heaven along with the Muslim martyrs? If not, then did they give up their lives in vain?

 Point 2: Mr. Ahsan used the words “(PBUH) after the names of Sk. Mujib and Gen. Zia. These words stand for: Peace Be Upon Him.”

 In my understanding, the phrase “Peace Be Upon Him” is reserved exclusively for Muhammad, the Prophet of Islam. In another words, it means: Let there be God’s mercy on Muhammad. It can not be used for any other human being, no matter how high a position one might have held in his or her life.

 Before asking my question on the issue of PBUH, I need to state here another but relevant issue in order to let readers know why I am going to ask my question on this seemingly innocent-appearing Muslim wish for the forgiveness by God of their Prophet?

 Abu Lahab, an uncle of Muhammad, had harassed him on a number of occasions. He, in association with his wife, is also believed to have tried to harm him during his proselytizing campaigns in Mecca.

 Due to their ill-designs against the Prophet, all Muslims have been cursing the couple for 1,400 years, and they would continue to do so until the last day of our universe. God, being kind and merciful, revealed Sura Lahab (111) with the purpose of reminding Muslims of their duty to Him. In keeping with God’s wish, Muslims recite this sura in their daily prayers. This helps God in remembering the punishment He has already reserved for the couple. On the Day of Judgment, He would do exactly what He has said in the sura about doing to the couple to please Muhammad and his followers.

 Questions: Why Muslims must keep on seeking God’s mercy for Muhammad, when he is believed to have committed not a single crime or sin in his entire life? Or, was he such a sinner as to need the prayers of his followers to avoid the fire of hell on the Day of Resurrection?

 Point 3: Prof. Taj Hashmi wrote in his unquestionable scholarly treatise: ‘Since history is always subject to distortion, there is hardly anything surprising about what the BNP-led government has done with the documents of the Liberation War of Bangladesh.”

 Comment: It is true that history is always subject to distortion. The example is the history of Bangladesh. It is being distorted even at a time, when witnesses to the events that led to the creation of Bangladesh are still alive, and also, when many aspects of the history were recorded on tape and paper.

 Question: When people are distorting the modern history of Bangladesh, how can we say that the history of religions, as we read it today, is free from distortions? How or must we take the stories told about Moses, Jesus Christ and Muhammad at their face value?

 I would appreciate straight and genuine responses to the questions I have asked above. Thanks in anticipation to those who are willing to help me with their educated responses. I fervently appeal to the members of Mukto-mona, Vinnomot and News from Bangladesh and those who are not their members not to waste their valuable time on writing responses, if they know that what they are going to write are beyond logic, and that they would not be able to substantiate their responses, when they are subjected to critical analysis and arguments.

 

July 27, 2004
