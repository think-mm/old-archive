5 Years of Mukto-Mona

Land reforms dilemma

 Ilyas Khokhar

Published on February 13, 2007

 

In the twentieth century, most countries of the world have passed through the process of land reforms for it has been learnt from historical experience that progress would never be achieved until the old means of production were replaced by new ones.

The USSR is a classic example, not only boosting its agricultural production, but also enhancing its industrial output. For example, the total crop area of the USSR in 1913 was 105 million hectares and this increased to 136.9 million hectares in 1938. Likewise, industrial growth was boosted from 100 percent in 1913 to 908.8 percent in 1938. These startling economic achievements of the USSR were the result of new economic theories. As Joseph Stalin once said, “Theory, if it is genuine theory, gives practical workers the power of orientation, clarity of perspective and confidence in their work, faith in the victory of the cause” (Problems of Leninism by J. Stalin, Pages 612, 609, 301).

Old economic theories, for example the theory of equilibrium, the theory of spontaneity in socialist construction, and the theory of stability of small peasant farming were replaced by the progressive Marxist theories of expanded reproduction and large-scale socialist farming. State and cooperative farms were established which increased the crop area by 50 percent due to the fact that the peasants were transformed into a mighty force as they pooled their implements and became united in collective farms. Similarly, in Cuba land reform was among the chief directives of the revolutionary wave of 1959. Almost all large holdings were seized by the National Institute for Land Reform (INRA); a ceiling of 166 acres (67 hectares) was established, and tenants were given full ownership rights. In Asia, South Korea’s spectacular economic advance and Indian Punjab’s progress were based on land reforms and mass education.

Against this, the data released by the Agricultural Census Organisation of Pakistan revealed that the total farm area in the country in 1972 was 49.21 million acres, and increased by only by 1.29 million acres to 50.50 million acres in 2000. Likewise, the total cultivated area increased by just 0.02 million acres, from 40.75 million acres in 1972 to 40.77 million acres in 2000.

The question is why our country, with a population of more than 65 percent in the villages and contributing 25 percent towards the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) from the agricultural sector, has lagged behind in agro-development. In this article I have tried to answer these questions.

The first land reforms were introduced in 1959, when the ceiling for private ownership of land was fixed at 500 acres irrigated and 1,000 acres un-irrigated land. However, this did little to distribute the land of the country’s prosperous rural elite among the poor. It was more a cosmetic exercise than a significant social change.

One of the problems was that ceilings were fixed in terms of individuals rather than families. The reforms included generous exemptions as well as separate provisions for orchards. Hardly 35 percent of the excess land declared by landowners was actually assumed by the government and the redistribution benefited only eight percent of the subsistence farmers.

Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, in 1972, made a second attempt at land reforms. This time the ownership ceiling was reduced to 150 acres of irrigated and 300 acres of un-irrigated land. The impact was not significant. Less than 0.9 million acres were acquired for redistribution, which was about one-third of the land resumed under the 1959 land reforms. Once again, land ceilings were based on individual holdings. As a result a number of large landowners managed to keep their holdings intact, and gave up only non-productive or swampy land.

In 1977, the Bhutto government further reduced ceilings on private ownership of farmland to about four hectares of irrigated land and about eight hectares of non-irrigated land. Additionally, agricultural income became taxable, though small farmers owning ten hectares or less were exempted. Then Chairman Federal Land Commission Sheikh Mohammad Rashid (deceased) worked very hard to implement these reforms, but the military regime of Ziaul Haq that ousted Bhutto failed to follow up on them. Governments in the 1980s and early 1990s avoided serious land reforms, because they drew much of their support from landowners. The last three land reforms managed to resume less than eight per cent of privately-owned land in the country, which was distributed amongst less than one per cent of the rural poor.

Land reforms are protected today by Article 23 of the Constitution, which states, “Every citizen shall have the right to acquire, hold and dispose of property in any part of Pakistan, subject to the Constitution and any reasonable restrictions imposed by law in the public interest.” In addition, the Shariat Appellate Bench of the Supreme Court has proclaimed land reforms against the tenets of Islam. Thus all lands acquired by feudals in return for their services rendered to the British Imperial power in 1857 or after until independence, were completely protected from land reforms.

According to a UN report, “Comprehensive land reforms are indispensable for any basic change in Pakistan’s political and economic system. Without such reforms, the nation may remain locked in a virtual political and economic paralysis. Periodic elections will bring little change, as many of the same people will be recycled through the legislatures, whatever their party labels or affiliations. Major economic reforms will keep waiting, as the feudal system generally believes in economic patronage rather than in good governance. Any enlightened social changes will be held hostage to the inherent conservatism of a feudal society.”

Such an important issue has been given no importance by the present regime and they even turned a deaf ear towards the advice of the World Bank that land reforms were vital for the development of Pakistan. The political influence of landlords has halted any further land reforms temporarily, but this does not mean that no progress is possible. The process of historic evolution tells us that progress never waits for particular events but gradually evolves with changes in the means of production. But can we wait for the slow process of evolution in this fast-growing world? No, we can’t wait! It is critical that the government acts immediately to implement land reforms in which earlier lacunas are removed.

On January 15, 2005, the federal cabinet rejected the amendment in the progressive clauses of the Land Reforms Act 1977 proposed by the Federal Land Commission (FLC). This indicates we have a reforms-friendly government but this is superficial: the government is still anti-tiller. According to Agricultural Statistics of Pakistan (1999-2000), 13 percent of the farms are below 0.5 hectares; 14 percent farms are between 0.5 to 1.0 hectares; 20 percent are between 1.0 to under 2.0 hectares; 46 percent farms are between 2.0 to 10.0 hectares; and only seven percent are above 10.0 hectares. In terms of area, the last group owns 40 percent of the total area and 27 percent own one per cent of the total farm area.

Balochistan presents a case of the highest level of unequal distribution. People owning 60 hectares and above are two percent and their average size of farms is 155 hectares. Since the land in Balochistan is not as fertile as in Punjab or Sindh, the situation is not so alarming there. In Punjab 37 percent own 10.0 hectares and above, and 36 percent in Sindh. This is a pathetic situation as it is hindering the country’s social, economic and political progress. Now it has been accepted even by the government that the increase in poverty is due to poor agriculture policies, as on March 21, 2006, a national daily reported that the National Assembly standing committee on food and agriculture held that flawed agriculture policies being pursued by the government have raised poverty levels.

So far as land reforms are concerned, the FLC revealed that only 1.8 million hectares (less than eight percent of arable land in the country) have been earmarked for redistribution so far. The military government has distributed 1.4 million hectares to some 288,000 small farmers and other beneficiaries. We have to struggle for more, because land ownership is still highly concentrated. More than half of the country’s total farmland is in farms of 50 acres or more.

It is apparent that the implementation of land reforms is impossible, for the class empowered to undertake these reforms comprises big landlords. Its class interests do not allow it to take any revolutionary measures in the farm sector. The obvious solution then, is an uprising of tillers, agriculture workers and tenants with the cooperation of industrial workers. In this way workers will decide their own fate and can take revolutionary measures to develop the farm and other sectors of society.
