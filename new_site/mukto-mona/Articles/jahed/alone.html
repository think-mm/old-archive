Can I do It Alone?

 Jahed Ahmed

 

Over the course of last few years, I remember, total number of fellow Bangalees with whom I had a chance to discuss several social-political issues related to my country, Bangladesh, will tantamount to a few hundreds, and almost in each cases both the person(s) with whom I discussed those issues and I, myself, reached basically the same conclusion- this country (Bangladesh) has no future! A profoundly pessimistic tone permeated in every such discussion I joined in. However, recently reading a small parable with a root in Sufi version  (a more accommodating version of Islam in contrast with Wahaabism) has struck my mind and made me reevaluate many of our easy-to-escape conclusions such as one mentioned above. The parable goes as follows: heavily disheartened and dejected by the chaos and grieves of the world, a man asked the Almighty – “God, you are said to be omnipotent, omniscient, yet so many injustice is happening to your creatures- killings, rapes, corruptions, unnecessary bloodsheds and what not! Can’t you do something?” A big silence continued and then came the reply from the God: “I did something. At least I created you.” After reading this parable, subject of my question was none but myself, and it was: if it so happened that each of us who are so fed up and frustrated about the existing realities of our societies had performed whatever small acts we were capable of, what an amazing

act that would have been! After all, Mahatma Gandhi, Iswar Chandra Vidya Sagar,  Sir Syed Ahmed,  Rabindranath Tagore, to name a few, are the names of individuals. They did not wait for a group to be formed before they performed their due duties individually. Why cannot we? 

 

Needless to say, governments systems in South Asian countries are far below standard in terms of service they provide to the public, transparency and accountability. The politicians, whether in power, or in opposition, are mostly corrupt and use political power more as a tool of making own fortunes than to serve the people. Illiteracy is profoundly felt in South Asian societies and, politicians, government officials at various levels take advantage of this phenomenon in deceiving people. The crucial question, however, is when we know- governments would not live up to our expectations, or for that our social-political infrastructures are not as developed as they are in West, what do we do? Do we have any alternatives? Or, should we give up even our individual roles? To answer this question, I feel tempted to cite a quote by Edward Everett Hale: I am only one, but still I am one. I cannot do everything, but still I can do something; and because I cannot do everything I will not refuse to do the something that I can do. Alas! Although, our intention is a noble one, in our criticism of an improper system, barely we remember that each of us has some roles to play in improving our society and its structure. We refuse to act because each of us has been well used to think, I cannot do anything alone.

 

  A few months ago, when I was living amid intense fears and uncertainty in the light of US Immigration & Naturalization Service (INS) having made it mandatory for immigrants of certain countries including Bangladesh to appear for Special Registration program. I lost my student status and hence all fears. What if a cop arrested me now and asked for legal documents?- was a common question I was living with. Exactly, at that time, a simple advertisement in a local paper from a human rights organization, I remember, created a profound sense of security, hope and self-dignity in my mind. The ad informed me that even if I don’t have legal status in this country (USA), I am, however, entitled to certain rights. For example- in New York City, a cop is not allowed to ask for legal documents to an illegal immigrant. I am not obliged to answer questions raised by an INS officer without the legal representation of a lawyer. So knowing my rights boosted my morale at a time of distress. What could be the implication of know-your-rights paradigm in a society such as ours? 

 

   Legend has it- charity begins at home. First thing in averting prevailing corruption, injustice in any society, in my opinion, is to educate oneself about one’s rights as a citizen. If I do not know what rights I am entitled to as a responsible citizen of a country, it is impossible to know when I am deprived of my basic rights. And under such situations, protesting a corrupt action committed by someone is beyond question. Knowing my rights and responsibility would allow me build a safeguard against violations of such rights, whether it’s me, or someone else, is the victim. Let me illustrate it with an example. In US, after a patient is registered in any hospital, a small brochure is handed over to each patient describing the patient’s rights such as rights to know in detail the process of treatments, diagnosis, medications and their possible side effects. If it so occurs that a patient suffers serious side effects due to doctor’s, or any other hospital personnel’s negligence, a patient can sue the doctor /hospital for malpractice, or wrong treatment. This has reduced the incidents of medical malpractice significantly in USA compared to any South Asian country. When even a seriously ill patient knows his/her rights to receive proper treatment, it naturally results in a mental boost of confidence and dignity stemming from the thought- by virtue of being sick one does not become simply a burden on doctors, or hospital staff. At the same time, doctors/nurses also treat a patient with more responsibility and care. Of course, patients also have certain responsibilities such as abiding by doctor’s advices, taking medications regularly and so on. With knowledge about some basic laws one can also help others when a violation occurs. Let me share a true incident I read in an American newspaper sometime ago. With the intention of pure harassment a racist cop once in New York City blocked the way of an African American and searched his body in the presence of dozens of passersby. The suspect turned out to be an innocent elderly person. Among those eye witnessing the real life drama was one person who knew it well that the cop had a bad motive behind the search and what he did was totally unlawful. He later met the victim and informed him that the cop could be sued since in accordance with New York state laws, a cop can search a private person only under three circumstances: on the basis of consent of the person to be searched, on the ground of a probable cause, and if the cop has a search warrant. None of these three conditions applied to above-mentioned case. Therefore, the cop was sued. The victim ultimately won the case while the cop was fined and temporarily suspended; hence it became news. The message is simple- if we know the laws, we can easily detect lawbreakers.

 

One might argue- what is applicable to American society not necessarily has any pertinence in societies of third world countries such as ours. I would humbly disagree. True that in terms of accountability, transparency, work ethics, our societies are not yet as developed as their Western counterparts, however, that is not enough as a reason for us to give up our own responsibilities. I remember, the former chief election commissioner of India, Mr. T N Seshan, once commented: “ A bad leader is not elected by bad people. A bad leader is elected because good people do not show up during election.” In our society, we tend to be more interested in cursing the system than performing own individual duties, thus making the job of fighting corruption and injustice even more difficult. Let us consider a generic example of corruption. Say, the officer-in charge (O.C.) of local police station is a corrupt person. He accepts bribes to spare criminals from arrests and even if he arrests, he releases them on acceptance of bribe. What should be the role of a private citizen under such circumstances? Is it- keeping quite thinking since one cannot change the corrupt system of whole country, what’s the use in protesting an isolated incident? Let us assume, someone didn’t protest which often happens in our societies. Now let us try to figure out what one could have done. At the minimum level, one could have done things like informing another responsible citizen about the corruption, who could write a letter to the local, or/and national newspapers, or could have had someone do the same. Such measures may not appear as a drastic method to stop the corruption overnight, yet no one could argue- it’s much better to do something than doing nothing. If the publication of a letter in a newspaper from a private citizen fails to draw the attention of the concerned authority, it would, however, encourage other citizens to report the incidents of corruption in a similar manner. There we go! After knowing our rights and responsibilities as a private citizen and those of government officials, it becomes easier to detect corruption and injustice resulting from the violations, but detection is of no use if we cannot take some steps as a preventive measures. And that’s the reporting: informing other responsible sources about the occurrence of corruptions. Although not mentioned in my example, it goes without saying, more often than not, a non-government officials such as a public representative could be also a corrupt and dishonest figure. Once detected, such occurrence should be reported as well, probably with even greater importance since when a leader is corrupt, public has no place to turn to, a very unfortunate yet prevalent situations in our societies.

 

    Summing up what I have tried to say above, the roles an individual, or a small group can play in averting corruption and injustice in our society is, to put them stepwise, as follows: educate yourself and others about your rights as a responsible citizen, know the extent, limitations and restrictions of power of those you want to fight, detect abuse of power if any, and speak out, protest and report. It might be relevant to quote Nobel Laureate economist Amartya Sen who, after studying famine in several developing countries including Bangladesh, has pointed out that there is a strong correlation between the freedom of press and level of famine in any developing society. What Sen meant is that it has been observed during many famines in developing countries- population died mainly not because there was lack, or shortage of food as we were told, but because people lack money to buy food since food price goes up due to hoardings. It’s like being in the ocean yet with not a single drop of potable water. No doubt, incidents of illegal hoardings and price of food beyond reach are unlikely to be reported by newspapers if they don’t have freedom, which is seen in any authoritarian regime. On the other hand, when press has enough freedom to report injustice and corruption, government cannot easily get away with the responsibility to account for such incidents.  

 

 

 

       I remember of having been advised by one of my school teachers in Bangladesh: If you eye witness an evil, try to resist, if you cannot, protest, if even that is not possible for you, at least, tell yourself- how much you dislike it; but please, don’t do nothing! It reminds me of a true experience of a German philosopher (I don’t recall his name at the moment) belonging to Hitler’s era. The philosopher, at the beginning of Hitler’s autocratic and anti-Jews policy, didn’t protest thinking he was not a Jew, but a proud German Catholic Christian, as was Hitler himself. The Hitler’s oppression continued and at one point his victims included not only just Jews, but also all those who believed in freedom and democracy. Alas! By the time, the philosopher raised his voice, there was no one to join him. The moral of the story is that each of us must not wait in the face of corruption and injustice until we become the victims ourselves. It should not happen that by the time we realize we should have done something, we might lose the ability to do whatever little things we are capable of! And vision for a developed, harmonic and progressive society for ourselves and for our next generations is not simply a dream. It’s a basic right. It’s a responsibility too.

 

(The end)
