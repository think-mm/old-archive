Re: What caused the death? Tsunami or the society 

Mohammad Asghar

Expressing his thoughts on the measures, which if taken, could have saved, if not all, but many lives from the devastating effects of the tsunamis that struck eleven Asian countries a few days ago, Dr. Biplab Pal, wrote in Mukto-mona: “Technolgy is advancing at rapid space. We can measure phenomena of nano-second, nano-meter, nanogram much more easily than ever. Building up a sensor network for Tsunami is not big deal by todays technology standards.”

 

Dr. Pal’s thoughts are noble, and they deserve attention of all those who are charged with the wellbeing of mankind. For his information, however, I need to state the following:

 

Scientists have already developed technology that allows them to scan the floors of the oceans in order to detect the emerging signs of tsunamis in them. Based in Hawaii, scientists monitor the Pacific Ocean round the clock. They have been concentrating on the Pacific because it is thought to be the main center of tsunamis. They are of the view that with the technology they have, they can save almost all the residents of Hawaii from death or injuries, if tsunamis were to hit them any time soon.

 

The countries which face the Indian Ocean never thought that tsunamis could ever strike them so hard. Based on this belief, they never thought of installing monitoring equipment on their soils. The result of this negligence is before our eyes: Tsunamis beat them and caused havocs of unimaginable magnitude, to recover from which, the affected countries will take at least a number of years.

 

Apart from lacking imagination, the leaders of many Asian countries, including Bangladesh, suffer from a unique infliction of their own. It is well known to almost all of us that if an earthquake of 8.9 magnitude on the Richter Scale ever struck Bangladesh, the destruction it is likely to cause cannot be imagined.  Despite this chilling forecast, Bangladesh chose to have only one Earthquake Observatory Station; that, too, after wasting over thirty years to put it together, whereas it ought to have at least three such stations for better monitoring of the earth’s movements.

 

Bangladesh cannot have more stations, because it does not have enough money to pay for them. It does not have enough money to spend on measures that would save its citizens’ lives, yet it is more than willing to let them spend over 300 million Dollars on Hajj and Qurbani the next year, even though their religion does not require them to do so.

 

If the people of Bangladesh do not want to invest money in measures that are likely to save their own lives in the event of being struck by a major calamity, would it be fair for us to blame the scientists or the capitalistic societies of the world for the death of Bangladeshi people?  We need to think hard on our own conducts; for, if we do not know what we are doing, in that case, we should not expect the rest of the world to do everything for us, and for ever.

 

Regards,

Mohammad Asghar
